\section{Summary}

\subsection{Motivation}

In many markets, firms supply differentiated products and compete in prices, while consumers possess incomplete information about how well each product matches their preferences. A central question for platforms (e.g., Amazon, Expedia, insurance comparison websites) and regulators is how to design the information environment that consumers face.

The key tension is immediate: more transparency improves product-consumer matching, but it also softens price competition by giving firms greater market power. Less transparency forces price competition --- products are perceived as near-perfect substitutes --- but consumers may end up purchasing a poorly matched product.

Armstrong and Zhou formalize this trade-off. They ask: within the class of all feasible signal structures, which one maximizes industry profit, and which one maximizes consumer surplus? Their answer reveals that the interests of firms and consumers are clearly opposed in how information should be structured, yet both sides agree that information should be symmetric across firms.

\subsection{Model Essentials}

Two symmetric firms ($i = 1,2$) supply differentiated products at zero cost. A risk-neutral consumer values product $i$ at $v_i \geq 0$; only her relative preference $x \equiv v_1 - v_2$ matters. Let $F$ denote the prior CDF of $x$, symmetric on $[-1,1]$, with density $f$. Two key parameters summarize the prior:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item $\mu \equiv E[v_i]$: expected value of a random product.
    \item $\delta \equiv E_F[\max\{x,0\}] = \int_{-1}^{0} F(x)\,dx$: expected match-quality gain from buying the preferred product over a random one.
\end{itemize}

Before buying, the consumer observes a private signal $s$ from a signal structure $\{\sigma(s|x), S\}$ chosen by a designer ---this is common knowledge. She updates to a posterior; since she is risk-neutral, only the posterior mean ($E[x \mid s]$) matters. Let $G$ denote the CDF of this posterior mean --- the posterior distribution. Firms observe $G$ but not $s$, and set prices simultaneously (Bertrand--Nash). The consumer buys from firm~1 if $E[x \mid s] > p_1 - p_2$.

The only constraint on $G$ is Bayesian consistency: $G$ must be a mean-preserving contraction (MPC) of $F$\footnote{, i.e.,
$$\int_{-1}^{x} G(\tilde{x})\,d\tilde{x} \;\leq\; \int_{-1}^{x} F(\tilde{x})\,d\tilde{x} \qquad \forall\, x \in [-1,1].$$}.
Any such $G$ can be generated by some signal structure. So instead of optimizing over signals, the authors optimize directly over posteriors --- a key simplification.

Lemma 1 shows that a posterior $G$ can sustain symmetric equilibrium price $p$ iff $G$ lies between two bounds derived from each firm's no-deviation condition\footnote{Note this is only in pure strategies.}:
$$L_p(x) \;\leq\; G(x) \;\leq\; U_p(x)$$

\section{Extension: Platform as Information Designer}
Here we could provide more intuitively, without the math, the intuition behind two models. Why and what would change and then how would the predictions look like in this new setting. 
\subsection{Motivation}

Armstrong and Zhou note that ``it would be valuable to embed this analysis within a framework in which the `information designer' is modeled explicitly as an economic agent'' (Section~IV, p.~562). In their paper, signal structures are chosen to maximize firm profit or consumer surplus, but no agent actually performs this optimization. A natural candidate is a \textbf{platform} --- Amazon, Expedia, an insurance comparison site --- that controls how much product information consumers see and monetizes through fees charged to firms or consumers.

The question is simple: \emph{what signal structure would a profit-maximizing platform choose, and how does its fee structure shape that choice?} Using the paper's existing toolkit --- the bounds $L_p$, $U_p$, the MPC constraint, and the feasible set from Proposition~3 --- we can trace through the answer under three fee regimes. The results suggest that the platform's business model is not a distributional detail: it determines the entire information environment.

\subsection{Setup}

We add a monopoly platform to the Armstrong--Zhou duopoly. The timing is:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item The platform commits to a signal structure (posterior $G$) and a fee schedule.
    \item Firms observe $G$ and the fees, decide whether to participate, and set prices simultaneously.
    \item The consumer observes her private signal $s$, pays the price (plus any per-unit fee), and buys one product.
\end{enumerate}
Everything else is unchanged: two symmetric firms, zero marginal cost, prior $F$ on $[-1,1]$, consumer always buys. We consider three fee instruments:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Per-transaction fee $\tau$}: the platform collects $\tau$ on every sale.
    \item \textbf{Lump-sum listing fee $\phi$}: each firm pays $\phi$ upfront to be listed.
    \item \textbf{Mixed fees $(\tau, \phi)$}: the platform uses both instruments simultaneously.
\end{itemize}

For each case, the platform chooses $(G, \text{fees})$ to maximize its revenue, subject to: (i)~$G$ is an MPC of $F$, (ii)~$G$ lies in $[L_{p}, U_{p}]$ for some symmetric equilibrium price $p$, (iii)~each firm's participation constraint holds (net profit $\geq 0$), and (iv)~the consumer's participation constraint holds ($CS \geq 0$).

\subsection{Pass-Through of Per-Unit Fees}

A key observation simplifies the per-unit case. With fee $\tau$, each firm's effective cost is $\tau$. Define the markup $\tilde{p}_i \equiv p_i - \tau$. Since the consumer's choice depends only on the price \emph{difference} $p_1 - p_2 = \tilde{p}_1 - \tilde{p}_2$, and each firm maximizes $\tilde{p}_i \cdot q_i(\tilde{p}_1 - \tilde{p}_2;\, G)$, the pricing game in markups is identical to the original zero-cost game. The no-deviation bounds $L_p, U_p$ depend only on the price difference and the symmetric markup level, so they are unchanged. The equilibrium markup is therefore $\tilde{p} = p(G)$, the consumer pays $p(G) + \tau$, and each firm's gross profit remains $p(G)/2$ regardless of $\tau$. The per-unit fee is \textbf{fully passed through} to consumers --- a consequence of inelastic total demand and price-difference-based allocation.

\subsection{Case 1: Per-Unit Fees Only}

The platform earns $\tau$ per sale ($= \tau$ total, since one unit is always sold). It wants $\tau$ as large as possible. The binding constraint is consumer participation. Under posterior $G$ and fee $\tau$, the consumer's surplus is:
$$CS \;=\; \underbrace{\mu + \int_{-1}^{0} G(x)\,dx}_{\text{expected gross utility}} \;-\; \underbrace{\big(p(G) + \tau\big)}_{\text{total price paid}}.$$
Setting $CS = 0$ (extracting all consumer surplus) gives the platform's optimal fee:
$$\tau^{*} \;=\; \mu + \int_{-1}^{0} G(x)\,dx \;-\; p(G).$$
The right-hand side is exactly $CS(G)$ from the original Armstrong--Zhou game. So the platform's problem reduces to: \emph{choose $G$ to maximize consumer surplus in the baseline game}. By Proposition~2 of the paper, the solution is the consumer-optimal signal $G^{**} = \min\{F,\, U_{p^{**}}\}$ with $p^{**} \approx 0.05\, p_F$.

\textbf{Outcome:} the consumer-optimal signal is implemented, but consumers get \emph{zero} surplus --- $\tau$ extracts it all. Firms earn small positive markups $p^{**}/2$. Mismatch occurs, so total welfare falls below $\mu + \delta$.

\subsection{Case 2: Lump-Sum Fees Only}

The platform charges $\phi$ per firm upfront. Since $\phi$ does not enter marginal cost, it does not affect pricing or the consumer's purchase decision. Each firm participates if and only if its gross profit covers the fee: $p(G)/2 \geq \phi$. The platform sets $\phi^* = p(G)/2$ and earns $2\phi^* = p(G)$.

The platform's problem reduces to: \emph{choose $G$ to maximize industry profit $p(G)$}. By Proposition~1 of the paper, the solution is the firm-optimal signal $G^* = L_{p^*}$ with $p^* = 2\delta/(1 - \log 2) \approx 6.5\,\delta$.

\textbf{Outcome:} the firm-optimal signal is implemented. No mismatch --- total welfare is maximized at $\mu + \delta$. Consumers retain surplus $CS = \mu + \delta - p^* > 0$. (For the uniform prior: $\mu = 2$, $\delta = 1/4$, $p^* \approx 1.63$, so $CS \approx 0.62$.)

\subsection{Case 3: Mixed Fees}

When the platform can charge both $\tau$ and $\phi$, both participation constraints bind at the optimum: $\tau$ extracts all consumer surplus and $\phi$ extracts all firm profit. The platform's total revenue is:
\begin{align*}
\Pi_{\text{platform}} &= \tau + 2\phi \\
&= \underbrace{\bigg(\mu + \int_{-1}^{0} G(x)\,dx - p(G)\bigg)}_{\tau^* = CS(G)} + \underbrace{p(G)}_{2\phi^* = p(G)} \\
&= \mu + \int_{-1}^{0} G(x)\,dx.
\end{align*}
The equilibrium price $p(G)$ cancels. The platform's revenue depends only on the match efficiency $\int_{-1}^{0} G(x)\,dx$, which is maximized at $\delta$ when $G$ induces no mismatch. Any signal that ensures the consumer buys her preferred product achieves this --- including full disclosure ($G = F$) or the firm-optimal signal ($G = L_{p^*}$). The platform earns $\mu + \delta$, which equals maximum total welfare. Both consumers and firms are left with zero surplus.

\textbf{Outcome:} the platform becomes the residual claimant on total welfare and therefore maximizes efficiency. The signal choice is pinned down only up to the no-mismatch condition --- the specific posterior does not matter, only that the consumer always buys her preferred product. (For the uniform prior: platform revenue $= 2.25$, $CS = 0$, firm profit $= 0$.)

\subsection{Comparison and the Fee-Structure Paradox}

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{l c c c}
\toprule
 & \textbf{Per-unit $\tau$} & \textbf{Lump-sum $\phi$} & \textbf{Mixed $(\tau, \phi)$} \\
\midrule
Platform maximizes & CS & Industry profit & Total welfare \\
Signal chosen & $G^{**}$ (cons-opt) & $L_{p^*}$ (firm-opt) & Any no-mismatch $G$ \\
Mismatch? & Yes & No & No \\
Total welfare & $< \mu + \delta$ & $= \mu + \delta$ & $= \mu + \delta$ \\
Consumer surplus & $0$ & $\mu + \delta - p^* > 0$ & $0$ \\
Firm profit (net) & $p^{**}/2 > 0$ & $0$ & $0$ \\
Platform revenue & $\text{CS}(G^{**})$ & $p^*$ & $\mu + \delta$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The most striking result is the \textbf{fee-structure paradox}: consumers are best off under lump-sum fees, which induce the \emph{highest} equilibrium prices. The mechanism is that the platform captures surplus from whichever side it taxes. Under per-unit fees, it taxes consumers, so it designs information to maximize consumer surplus --- then takes it all. Under lump-sum fees, it taxes firms, so it designs information to maximize firm profits --- and consumers keep the untaxed residual $\mu + \delta - p^*$. The ``consumer-friendly'' fee structure (commissions) leads to worse consumer outcomes than the ``firm-friendly'' one (listing fees).

\subsection{Takeaways}

\textbf{The business model shapes information design.} The platform's fee structure is not a mere distributional detail --- it determines which signal the platform wants to implement. Per-unit fees align the platform's incentive with consumer surplus (which it then extracts); lump-sum fees align it with firm profit. Mixed fees align it with total welfare. This echoes a theme from Rochet and Tirole (2003): in two-sided markets, the \emph{structure} of fees, not just their level, has allocative consequences.

\textbf{Regulatory implications.} A regulator who cannot directly control information design might regulate the fee structure instead. Mandating lump-sum fees indirectly implements the welfare-maximizing signal \emph{and} leaves consumers with positive surplus. Allowing unrestricted mixed fees achieves efficiency but leaves consumers with nothing. Mandating per-unit fees is the worst option: consumers get zero surplus \emph{and} total welfare is reduced by mismatch.

\textbf{Limitations.} This sketch assumes a monopoly platform with full commitment. With competing platforms, fees would be driven down and information design incentives would weaken. Without commitment (the platform adjusts $G$ after observing prices), the timing game changes fundamentally. Finally, if the platform chooses its own fee structure, it picks mixed fees --- achieving efficiency but extracting all surplus.

\section{Mathematical Details for the Platform Extension}

This appendix collects the step-by-step calculations behind the results in Section~3. All notation follows Armstrong and Zhou (2022): $F$ is the prior CDF of $x = v_1 - v_2$ on $[-1,1]$; $G$ is the posterior CDF; $p(G)$ is the symmetric equilibrium price under posterior $G$ with zero cost; $\mu = E[v_i]$; $\delta = \int_{-1}^{0} F(x)\,dx$; $CS(G) = \mu + \int_{-1}^{0}G(x)\,dx - p(G)$.

\subsection{Pass-Through of Per-Unit Fees}

With per-unit fee $\tau \geq 0$, firm $i$'s profit is $(p_i - \tau)\, q_i(p_1 - p_2;\, G)$. Define the markup $m_i \equiv p_i - \tau$. Then:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Firm $i$'s profit $= m_i \cdot q_i(m_1 - m_2;\, G)$, since $p_1 - p_2 = m_1 - m_2$.
    \item The demand function $q_i$ depends on $G$ and the price difference only.
    \item Firm $i$'s first-order condition in $m_i$ is identical to the zero-cost first-order condition in $p_i$.
\end{itemize}

For the no-deviation bounds, recall that in the original game, firm~2's no-deviation condition requires $p_2'\, G(p_1 - p_2') \leq p \cdot G(0) = p/2$ for all $p_2'$. With cost $\tau$, this becomes $(p_2' - \tau)\, G(p_1 - p_2') \leq (p - \tau)/2$ for all $p_2'$. Substituting $m_2' = p_2' - \tau$ and $m = p - \tau$:
$$m_2'\, G(m + \tau - m_2' - \tau) = m_2'\, G(m - m_2') \leq m/2.$$
This is the same bound as in the zero-cost game with symmetric markup $m$. Therefore, the equilibrium bounds $L_p, U_p$ apply to the markup $m = p(G)$, the equilibrium retail price is $p(G) + \tau$, and each firm's profit is $p(G)/2$.

\subsection{Platform's Problem: Per-Unit Fees}

The platform chooses $G$ and $\tau$ to maximize $\tau$, subject to:
\begin{align}
    &G \text{ is an MPC of } F, \label{eq:mpc}\\
    &L_{p(G)}(x) \leq G(x) \leq U_{p(G)}(x) \quad \forall\, x, \label{eq:bounds}\\
    &\underbrace{\mu + \int_{-1}^{0} G(x)\,dx - p(G) - \tau}_{\text{consumer surplus}} \geq 0, \label{eq:consumer_ir}\\
    &p(G)/2 \geq 0. \label{eq:firm_ir_tau}
\end{align}
Constraint~\eqref{eq:firm_ir_tau} is trivially satisfied. Constraint~\eqref{eq:consumer_ir} binds at the optimum (the platform extracts as much as possible):
$$\tau^* = \mu + \int_{-1}^{0} G(x)\,dx - p(G) = CS(G).$$
The platform's problem becomes $\max_G\, CS(G)$ subject to \eqref{eq:mpc}--\eqref{eq:bounds}. This is exactly the consumer's problem in Armstrong--Zhou. By their Proposition~2, the solution is:
$$G^{**}(x) = \min\big\{F(x),\; U_{p^{**}}(x)\big\} \quad \text{for } x < 0,$$
with $p^{**}$ determined by $\gamma \approx 0.05$ as in the paper.

\textbf{Numerical check (uniform prior):} $\mu = 2$, $\delta = 1/4$, $p_F = 1$, $p^{**} \approx 0.05$. The consumer-optimal CS in the baseline game is approximately $\mu - p^{**} + (\text{match value under } G^{**}) \approx 2.20$. The platform sets $\tau^* \approx 2.20$ and earns this amount. Firms earn $p^{**}/2 \approx 0.025$ each.

\subsection{Platform's Problem: Lump-Sum Fees}

The platform chooses $G$ and $\phi$ to maximize $2\phi$, subject to:
\begin{align}
    &G \text{ is an MPC of } F, \label{eq:mpc2}\\
    &L_{p(G)}(x) \leq G(x) \leq U_{p(G)}(x) \quad \forall\, x, \label{eq:bounds2}\\
    &\mu + \int_{-1}^{0} G(x)\,dx - p(G) \geq 0, \label{eq:consumer_ir2}\\
    &p(G)/2 - \phi \geq 0. \label{eq:firm_ir_phi}
\end{align}
Constraint~\eqref{eq:firm_ir_phi} binds: $\phi^* = p(G)/2$, so revenue $= p(G)$. The problem becomes $\max_G\, p(G)$ subject to \eqref{eq:mpc2}--\eqref{eq:bounds2}. This is the firm's problem in Armstrong--Zhou. By their Proposition~1:
$$G^* = L_{p^*}, \qquad p^* = \frac{2\delta}{1 - \log 2}.$$
Since $G^* = L_{p^*}$ induces no mismatch, $\int_{-1}^{0}G^*(x)\,dx = \delta$, and consumer surplus is:
$$CS = \mu + \delta - p^* = \mu + \delta - \frac{2\delta}{1 - \log 2} = \mu - \frac{(1 + \log 2)\,\delta}{1 - \log 2}.$$
This is positive whenever $\mu > \frac{(1+\log 2)}{(1-\log 2)}\,\delta \approx 5.5\,\delta$. Under the paper's maintained assumption $V > 2$ (which implies $\mu \geq 2$), and since $\delta \leq 1/4$ for the uniform prior, this holds easily: $5.5 \times 0.25 = 1.375 < 2$.

\textbf{Numerical check (uniform prior):} $p^* \approx 1.63$, $\phi^* \approx 0.815$, platform revenue $\approx 1.63$, $CS = 2 + 0.25 - 1.63 = 0.62$.

\subsection{Platform's Problem: Mixed Fees}

The platform chooses $G$, $\tau$, and $\phi$ to maximize $\tau + 2\phi$, subject to \eqref{eq:mpc}--\eqref{eq:bounds} and:
\begin{align}
    &\mu + \int_{-1}^{0} G(x)\,dx - p(G) - \tau \geq 0, \label{eq:consumer_ir3}\\
    &p(G)/2 - \phi \geq 0. \label{eq:firm_ir_mixed}
\end{align}
Both constraints bind at the optimum: $\tau^* = \mu + \int_{-1}^{0}G(x)\,dx - p(G)$ and $\phi^* = p(G)/2$. Therefore:
\begin{align*}
\Pi_{\text{platform}} &= \tau^* + 2\phi^* \\
&= \Big(\mu + \int_{-1}^{0}G(x)\,dx - p(G)\Big) + p(G) \\
&= \mu + \int_{-1}^{0}G(x)\,dx.
\end{align*}
The price $p(G)$ drops out. The platform's revenue equals the consumer's expected gross utility, which is maximized when $\int_{-1}^{0}G(x)\,dx = \delta$ (no mismatch). The maximum revenue is $\mu + \delta$.

Any posterior with no mismatch achieves this: $G = F$ (full disclosure), $G = L_{p^*}$ (firm-optimal), or any $G$ such that the consumer buys her preferred product with probability one. The platform is indifferent among all of them.

\textbf{Numerical check (uniform prior):} $\Pi_{\text{platform}} = 2 + 0.25 = 2.25$. Under full disclosure ($p_F = 1$): $\tau^* = 2 + 0.25 - 1 = 1.25$, $\phi^* = 0.5$, total $= 1.25 + 1.0 = 2.25$. \checkmark
